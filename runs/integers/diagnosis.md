# Diagnosis

## The wrong act
The child sees two numbers — 5 and 3 — and piles them together: "five things
and three things makes eight things." They treat the signs as invisible
decoration and just count everything in front of them. Or, if they do subtract,
they compute 5 − 3 = 2 but have no sense of *why* subtracting is the right
move or which direction the answer goes — they're following a half-remembered
rule with no physical backing.

The physical action: picking up two groups of stuff and dumping them into one
pile, then counting the whole pile. The minus sign on the 3 doesn't change
anything they *do* — it's just a mark on the page.

## The forbidden representation
Two separate groups of countable discrete objects (five dots/blocks and three
dots/blocks) shown side by side. This layout makes "count them all → 8" the
most natural, available action. Any arrangement where +5 appears as five
individual things and −3 appears as three more individual things is forbidden —
it invites the child to merge the two collections and count the total.

## The foothold
The child already understands *direction*. They know that walking forward five
steps and then walking backward three steps does not put you eight steps away.
They feel, pre-mathematically, that going backward undoes some of going forward
— you end up *closer* to where you started, not farther. "Forward" and
"backward" are real, felt concepts. The child also perceives *where they are
standing* — they can point to the spot they ended up and see that it is past
the start but not as far as they went initially. This directional, positional
sense is the correct perception to build on.
