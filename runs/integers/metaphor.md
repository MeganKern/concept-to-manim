# Metaphor

## Physical truth
When you walk forward and then walk backward, you end up closer to where you
started — not farther away. Where your feet stop is the answer.

## Primitive choice

**Primitive 2 — Position on a line** (composition: "Walk a distance").
A dot walks along a number line: forward for the positive part, backward for
the negative part. The answer is *where the dot stops* — a single position,
not a count.

Why this is the fewest: one primitive does it all. The number line gives
direction (forward vs. backward), position (where you are), and distance (how
far you went). No second primitive is needed because the physical situation — a
walk with a turnaround — is entirely captured by movement on a line.

No other primitives are used. Measurement overlay is unnecessary because the
number line's tick marks already serve as the ruler — the child reads the
landing spot directly.

## Referents

| Symbol in worked example | Physical referent on screen                          |
|--------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| 0 (start)                | The spot on the line where the dot begins standing    |
| +5                       | Walking forward 5 steps — an arrow from 0 to 5       |
| −3                       | Walking backward 3 steps — an arrow from 5 back to 2 |
| +2 (answer)              | Where the dot is standing when the walk is done       |

## Affordance gate

**Forbidden representation (from diagnosis):** two separate groups of countable
discrete objects shown side by side.

**Check:** Position-on-a-line shows a single traveler making a journey with two
legs. There are no two piles of objects to merge. The forward arrow and the
backward arrow point in *opposite directions* on the same path — the child
sees "go, then come back," not "here's a pile of five and a pile of three."
The visual affordance is *travel and direction*, which directly matches the
correct operation and makes the wrong act (dump both piles together and count)
physically unavailable. You cannot "add" a backward walk to a forward walk by
piling them — one undoes the other, and the child already feels that.

**Verdict:** Passes. The representation does not afford the forbidden act.
