# Phase 1 — DIAGNOSE

## The wrong act
The child sees two separate piles of numbers — a "top pile" (3 and 1) and a
"bottom pile" (4 and 8). They pick up the tops, smoosh them together (3 + 1 = 4),
then pick up the bottoms, smoosh those together (4 + 8 = 12). They're treating
the fraction bar as a shelf that keeps two independent stacks apart, and "adding
fractions" means adding each stack on its own. It's the same thing they'd do if
you said "add 3 apples and 1 apple, then add 4 oranges and 8 oranges" — combine
like with like.

## The forbidden representation
Any picture that shows a fraction as two separate countable groups — for
instance, 3 dots sitting above 4 dots, or a column of tally marks for the
numerator beside a column for the denominator. More broadly: any representation
where the numerator and denominator appear as independent, discrete, countable
things that can be picked up and combined separately. This includes showing a
fraction as "a row of 3 objects / a row of 4 objects." If the child can see two
distinct piles, the "add each pile" move feels natural and available.

Also forbidden: partitioned bars or grids where the child can count individual
cells in the numerator and denominator independently — this still affords
treating top-count and bottom-count as separate addition problems.

## The foothold
The child already understands *amount* — how much stuff there is. They know what
"almost full" looks like versus "just a little bit." They can see that 3/4 of a
glass is a lot, and 1/8 of a glass is a tiny splash. They can feel that when you
pour two amounts together, you get MORE — the level goes up. And if someone told
them the answer was 4/12 (which is 1/3 of a glass), they'd feel something was
off: "Wait, I started with almost a full glass and added more, but now I have
less than half? That's wrong." The child's sense of *continuous amount* — levels,
fills, how-much — is correct and strong. That's what we build on.
