# Phase 2 — GROUND

## Physical truth (one sentence, 6-year-old-acceptable)
When you pour two partly-full glasses into one glass, the liquid rises to
wherever the combined amount reaches — and you read how full it is with one
ruler, not two different rulers.

## Primitive choice

Three primitives, each with its reason:

1. **Container + Fill** — Two containers (tall bars) each holding a continuous
   level of liquid. The child sees each fraction as *how full the glass is* —
   a single, undivided amount — not as two separate countable numbers. This is
   the core representation: a fraction IS a level. Chosen because its affordance
   (continuous amount) directly blocks the wrong act (grabbing two separate
   piles). No internal division lines on the fill — ever.

2. **Measurement overlay** — Tick marks laid on the OUTSIDE of a container,
   like notches on a measuring cup. These arrive AFTER the child already sees
   and accepts the continuous level. They turn "this much" into a readable
   number. Crucially, re-marking the same container with finer ticks (quarters
   → eighths) shows that the amount doesn't change — only the ruler changes.
   This is the physical version of "find a common denominator": switch to the
   same ruler so you can read both amounts on one scale.

3. **Physical transfer** — The liquid from the small container pours into the
   large container. The level rises. Nothing is created or destroyed. The
   combined amount is right there — visible as the new level — before any
   number names it.

## Referents

| Symbol in worked example | Physical object or state                                                  |
|--------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 3/4                      | The level of liquid in container A — it sits high, almost full            |
| 1/8                      | The level of liquid in container B — a tiny splash near the bottom        |
| 4 (denominator of 3/4)   | The number of equally-spaced notches on container A's first ruler         |
| 8 (denominator of 1/8)   | The number of equally-spaced notches on container B's ruler               |
| "find a common denominator" | Replacing container A's 4-notch ruler with an 8-notch ruler (same container, same level, finer marks) |
| 6/8                      | The SAME level in container A, now read against the 8-notch ruler — the liquid reaches the 6th mark |
| 6/8 + 1/8                | Pouring container B's splash into container A; the level rises            |
| 7/8                      | The final level in container A after the pour — it reaches the 7th of 8 marks |

## Affordance gate

**Forbidden representation (from diagnosis):** Any picture where the numerator
and denominator appear as separate countable groups that can be picked up and
combined independently. Also forbidden: partitioned bars/grids with countable
cells.

**Check against chosen representation:**

- Container + Fill shows a *single continuous rectangle* of color inside a
  container. There are no cells, no separate piles, no "3 things on top and
  4 things on the bottom." The child cannot grab the numerator separately
  from the denominator because they aren't separate things — the level IS the
  fraction, whole and indivisible.

- Measurement overlay adds tick marks to the OUTSIDE of the container, like
  notches on a measuring cup. These do not partition the fill into countable
  cells — the fill stays as one unbroken rectangle. The ticks are a ruler
  laid alongside a continuous amount. The child reads "the liquid reaches
  the 3rd mark out of 4" — they're reading a position on a scale, not
  counting discrete objects. There is no separable "numerator pile" to smoosh.

- Physical transfer moves liquid from one container into another. The child
  sees levels change — one goes down, the other goes up. At no point are
  there two separate countable groups that could be independently added.

**Verdict: PASS.** The chosen representation does not afford the child's wrong
act. The "add tops and bottoms separately" error requires seeing tops and
bottoms as separate graspable quantities, and this representation never
presents them that way.
